# Speaker Notes Guide

How to write speaker notes that turn a slide deck into a live performance.

## The Principle

The slides are the visual aid. The speaker is the story. The notes script the performance — not as a teleprompter to read from, but as a guide for what to say, when to pause, and when to reveal the framework behind what the audience just experienced.

## Note Structure

Every slide's notes follow this template:

```
TIMING: XX:XX

SAY: 'The narrative the speaker delivers. Written in conversational 
tone — how you'd actually say it, not how you'd write it. 2-4 
sentences maximum.'

[Optional] META-MOMENT: 'Notice what I just did? That was [framework 
name]. I [specific thing you did] before telling you the name, 
because [reason].'

TRANSITION: 'The bridge sentence that connects this slide to the 
next one.'

[Optional] AUDIENCE PULSE: Pause 3 seconds. / Ask: "How many of 
you have experienced this?" / Wait for hands.
```

## Meta-Moments

Meta-moments are the most powerful teaching tool in the deck. They work like this:

1. You **demonstrate** a principle through your delivery (use a story, use brevity, use structure)
2. Later, you **reveal** what you did: "Notice that the story I told you followed four stages — Setting, Dilemma, Action, Outcome. That's Klein's model. You experienced it before I named it."

The audience learns through experience, then gets the vocabulary. This is more memorable than definitions → examples.

**Guidelines for meta-moments:**
- Use 3-5 per presentation (more dilutes the effect)
- Space them throughout — not all in one section
- The reveal should feel like an "aha" — not a gotcha
- Connect back to the specific moment: "Remember when I said X? That was Y."

## Timing Cues

Use cumulative timing, not per-slide timing:

- TIMING: 0:00 (start)
- TIMING: 3:00 (after opening story)
- TIMING: 10:00 (end of Act I)
- etc.

This helps the speaker pace themselves against the clock. If they're at slide 15 and the clock says 30:00, they know they're on track for a 50-minute talk.

## Audience Pulse Points

Types of audience interaction:

1. **Raise of hands**: "How many of you have experienced this?" — low-risk, gets people physically engaged
2. **Turn-and-talk**: "Turn to your neighbor and explain X in 30 seconds" — high engagement, requires timer
3. **Live challenge**: "You have 60 seconds. Do X." — highest engagement, creates shared experience
4. **Rhetorical pause**: State something surprising. Pause 3 full seconds. Let it land.
5. **Check-in question**: "Before I move on — is the distinction between X and Y clear?"

Place 2-3 interaction beats per 50-minute lecture. More breaks the flow. Fewer makes it a monologue.

## Transition Phrases

Good transitions connect the last idea to the next one:

- "So that's clarity. But clarity without structure is still chaos."
- "You know what to say. Now let's talk about what to cut."
- "That's the theory. Let me show you what it looks like in practice."
- "One more concept, and then we'll close with your action plan."

Bad transitions are non-sequiturs:
- "Next slide."
- "Moving on..."
- "OK so now let's talk about..."

## Do's and Don'ts

**Do:**
- Write notes in conversational voice (contractions, short sentences)
- Include the exact story to tell, not a summary of it
- Note where to pause and for how long
- Script the meta-moments word for word

**Don't:**
- Write notes as bullet points to read from the slide
- Include everything the slide already shows (the audience can read)
- Script every word — leave room for natural delivery
- Forget the timing cues
